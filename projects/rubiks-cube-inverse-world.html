Rubik's Cube — Inverse World

Independent game · Surreal puzzle worlds · 2.5D and 3D spaces

Introduction

Rubik's Cube — Inverse World is an independent project inspired by the mysteries of the world—from Egyptian pyramids to optical illusions like the Penrose Stair, and experiments exploring altered perception. The game invites players into a surreal, Rubik's Cube-inspired universe where each level is an inverted world.

Players navigate inverted spaces, solve puzzles, and interact with distorted environments that blend 2D, 2.5D, and 3D aesthetics to evoke curiosity, confusion, and wonder.

Creating media

Interactive digital media, game design, 3D modeling, animation, and pixel art. Tools: Unity · Maya · Cinema 4D · Adobe Substance 3D Painter · Piskel

Inspiration

I love playing with the Rubik's Cube. I began imagining: what if each mini-cube were a small world, separate yet still connected? I see the Rubik's Cube as a reflection of myself—made of small stories: confusing, childlike, idealistic. A TED Talk by Donald Hoffman deepened my wonder, suggesting our senses are like simplified icons rather than true reality.

I was also inspired by inversion experiments, the Penrose Stairs, pyramids, and higher dimensions.

World design and meta-structure

The game is structured as interconnected worlds inside the Rubik's Cube. The Inverse World is guided by four principles: Physical inversion (flipped sky and ocean), Space paradox (impossible structures), Evolution (2.5D/2D to 3D), and Unrelenting discovery (no final truth).

Design process

I drew key scenes in Procreate, animated in Procreate Dream, modeled the glass Rubik's Cube in Cinema 4D, and built 2.5D and 3D assets in Maya and Unity. A glowing question mark symbolizes confusion and curiosity, acting as a key to unlock the next world.

Scene design

Scenes include the candy-colored Rubik's Cube World (2.5D), the Inverse World with green clouds and yellow ocean, the Penrose Stairs (2D), and the Pyramid Interior (3D) with Egyptian-inspired details and the Sphinx.

Mechanics and conclusion

I architected the core mechanics and led Unity programming. The final scene takes place in a higher dimension of the Rubik's Cube; placing the question mark does not provide final satisfaction—the search for understanding continues. The Rubik's Cube becomes a meta-structure where players can record their own stories.
