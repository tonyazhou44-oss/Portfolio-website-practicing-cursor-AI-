The Game — Pressgrow

Independent project · OpenProcessing game · Pressure, growth, and mental health

Introduction

Pressgrow is an independent game about stress, growth, and the invisible pressures placed on teenagers. It was inspired by a trip to Jiangyong in Hunan Province, where I observed children who had time to run through rivers and forests and play freely with friends—very different from the city teenagers I know.

That contrast made me realize how excessive the pressure on city children can be. I discovered the game Synthetic Big Watermelon, where small objects combine into larger ones. This mechanic resonated with how pressure accumulates. In Pressgrow, pressure balls synthesize on the left side, while a boy on the right grows bigger—eventually becoming seriously ill and depressed. The work is designed for exhibition to help people feel and think about this issue.

Concept and narrative

The game contrasts a natural landscape where time feels abundant, and a city reality where time is tightly scheduled. The PDF spreads include playful typography and phrases like "Travel" and "Country side" to emphasize the contrast between place, freedom, and expectation.

Mechanics: Stress feeding

On the left, players synthesize pressure balls; as two balls combine, they form a larger ball. On the right, a boy grows larger each time pressure increases. The boy's growth looks like "success" from the outside, but internally it represents accumulating strain that eventually leads to depression.

How to play

You control where the balls fall by moving your mouse and clicking to release. When two balls of the same type meet, they combine. As balls synthesize, the boy becomes bigger. Press S to "shake" stuck balls (costs 30 coins). You lose if you drop balls outside the allowed area or synthesize into a specific "end" ball.

Meaning and conclusion

You think you are helping the boy by giving him everything—but in the game, everything you feed him turns into pressure. The process is painful for him while the player experiences excitement. The guide closes with: "Thank you for reading. Have a nice day. Be happy and live your day." The game uses a simple mechanic to open a broader conversation about expectations and mental health.
